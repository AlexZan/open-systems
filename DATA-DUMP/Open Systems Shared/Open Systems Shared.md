#  Introduction to Open Democracy and Open Systems

This document will attempt to identify areas of global improvement and
provide solutions which will eventually lead to a proposal for a
democratized and decentralized system of collaboration. These systems
have no set use and can be used from small two person organizations to
global collaboration.\
\
I begin by identifying problems this system aims to solve.

# Democracy Origins

In the days of tribal hunter gatherers, the tribe was small enough that
anyone could have equal opportunity to voice their opinion when everyone
gathered to make decisions. This could be seen as a primitive form of
direct democracy.

At some point in history, two tribes began to cooperate and if they were
far enough from each other they would naturally select representatives
to travel the distance when decisions needed to be made instead of the
entire tribe going. This could be seen as a primitive form of
representative democracy.

# Representative Democracy

Unlike direct democracy, representative democracy involves individuals
electing other individuals to represent their best interests. There are
several issues with this type of democracy:

## Assumed Best Interest

There is no equal direct communication between the individuals and the
representative and so when the representative votes, they assume the
interest of the general public, and we assume they have our best
interest.

## Promised Changes and Position is Not Guaranteed

Typically we vote on representatives based on the set of proposed
changes and stance they define before they are elected. But there is no
guarantee that the representative needs to follow through with the
promises made.

## Conflict of Interest and Lobbying

A representative can have direct or indirect income sources such as
through businesses beyond their income as a representative, which could
interfere with their duty of making a decision with the population\'s
best intention in mind if it conflicts with a business or personal
interest. A very common and accepted practice that often results in
conflict of interest is lobbying.

## Campaign Costs

Part of the personal interest of the representative is to get reelected
if they are able to, this costs a great deal of time and money and is in
no service to the primary function of the representative but can
interfere with primary duties and might be another point of conflict of
interest.

# Direct Democracy

Unlike a Representative Democracy a Direct Democracy allows each
individual to vote on specific issues, not representatives. While it
solves the issues outlined in the Representative Democracy section, it
faces its own issues.

In 2021 the Swiss voted to increase the minimum wage to \$25 dollars.
While this appeared great to many of the voters, the negative economic
consequences of such a regulatory decision are mainly understood by
economists. But there is no mechanism for the Swiss populus to have an
open discussion with economists about such issues to understand the
consequences.

The problem with Direct Democracy is not everyone has experience or an
understanding in every subject that is being voted on, as a result, they
are inclined to vote on behalf of someone else\'s understanding they
trust to some degree, this creates an incentive to influence as many
individuals and votes.

In 1995 the Quebec referendum in Canada to split Canada vote was lost to
50.58% against the split. Can anyone confidently say that every
individual that voted, comprehensively understood the consequences of
such a vote? Yet the vote that would have had drastic consequences was
marginally denied by .58%. There were many leaders who understood many
of the consequences, and as a result took to the streets and media at
the time to influence votes in their favour. In situations such as this,
direct democracy seems more like an archaic form of representative
democracy without the electoral process, relying on those who are the
greatest influencers.

# Improvements

Instead of being influenced, ideally individuals should be inspired to
understand the issue being voted on. In an ideal scenario, opposing
sides would provide a joint information package that both sides agree
to, outlining the consequences of both options. This information package
would include references to the discussions and debates they had while
arriving at the conclusions for those consequences, with experts
weighing in with their best assessments for the accuracy of those
consequences. With references to support the points being argued. All
voters would then have equal access to all the information, to make a
more informed vote. The concept of an information package is an abstract
representation of a system that will be eventually proposed.

In an even more ideal scenario, a service for the population to have
open discussion on issues with experts, and each other, to crowd source
creative opinions, which would be part of the above information package.
This sort of system, though, would be a double edged sword, in that
crowdsourcing opinions might provide some new and creative ideas, but it
could also create a great deal of discussion messages that do not
provide value, which would have to be filtered or sifted through,
creating a potentially logistical conundrum in doing so. Ideally the
system would organically sift through discussion messages.

There have been several ideal scenarios and systems stated in this
document so far, and there will be more to come. This document attempts
to outline problems, and provide ideal scenarios, finally providing
solutions as systems that should result in the realization of many of
these ideal scenarios solving many of the problems the world currently
faces.

With the above ideal scenarios, where individuals have access to
comprehensive information packages, comes an important question: Just
because an individual has access to information packages, does that
guarantee they will choose to be informed before they vote?

A voter can still choose not to understand the consequences of a vote,
and not vote or instead be influenced by another individual. There are
many reasons for this, but mainly we are emotional beings, it is easier
to feel content with a vote if we believe it is correct, versus
understanding it is correct. Understanding requires time and mental
energy to learn the required information, which might contradict a
position we feel content with requiring even more energy to overcome. Or
it might not be an interesting subject to the voter, but they have a
sense of trust in the influencer and so they vote in their favor.

It seems even with the previous improvements, the influencer incentive
is still there. Maybe the idea then, is to gradually decrease it, by
gradually increasing the amount of individuals that inform themselves.
As stated above, this depends on having the comprehensive information
readily available, but also the motivation to digest the information and
participate in the democratic system to begin with. More on this later.

Open Democracy

*The principles, systems and concepts I propose here are just that, only
proposals. I expect many will change. As long as this document helps
create principals, systems and concepts which will help sustain the fair
and collaborative process of ever-long change.*

The key proposed difference between previous forms of democracy and open
democracy is that no individual has any inherent initial form of voting
power, but all individuals have equal opportunity to increase voting
power.

Another key difference is that open democracy is a decentralized form of
democracy, which I propose is a form of a democracy that is not
dependent on any form of centralization, as are all previous non
primitive forms of democracy, such as the centralized leger of citizens
and the more modern form of identification and centralized
identification validation for voting. Achieving this has not been
possible until the last decade since guaranteed decentralized trust was
impossible until the advent of blockchain and immutable leger
technology. As such these forums of democracy require these digital
technologies, just like current forms of democracy require ballots.

I propose that an individual\'s voting power can only be increased by
that individual creating value for others. *The use of the word creating
is important, instead of contributing, since it implies something new
must be created, not just contributed as a monetary fund. As it will be
described later, there is no way to financially increase power.*

The value of a creation is determined by all voting individuals.
Individuals vote to assess the value of a creation, and the more value
that a creation was determined to have by voters, the more voting power
the creator achieves.

A creation can be anything that can be documented in an immutable leger,
such as a discussion forum. The creation itself can be a text post, an
image, video, proof of some work or proof of some tangible creation,
etc.

There is no absolute voting power, it is specific to a subject. E.g.
Someone that created value in #technology gets voting power in
#technology. I propose the \# symbol is used to indicate a subject tag
in documentation. Anyone can create value in any subject(s) of their
choosing, and anyone can vote on the value of a creation in the
subject(s) of their choosing.

I propose the value of voting power an individual has should be
expressed as experience. Since they created value in a specific subject
in the past, they now have experience in that subject. Experience is
greater than knowledge alone since knowledge can go unused, while
experience requires both knowledge and motivation; taking action to
apply it in creating value.

Experience is consumed. When an individual votes with their experience,
the experience they voted with is consumed. *This means that an
individual is likely to vote on meaningful things to them, things that
will provide most value in their opinion.*

Downvoting consumes an individual\'s experience to remove experience
from another individual. This can never be performed directly to an
individual, but only through a documented creation that is deemed as
being harmful in the voters opinion. *This results in always providing a
documented reason for the upvote or downvote. Creating an immutable
narrative of the flow of voting power and value.*

An individual has as much voting power as they have experience; an
individual can vote with 10 experience on a creation, or vote 10 times
with 1 experience on a creation, there is no difference. This is a
significant point as it shows there is no importance on identity, since
a user can create as many accounts to vote with as they desire since it
will provide no benefit as they would need to create the equivalent
experience across all accounts or just one. *This concept of identity
having no relevance in function has widespread effects on the system
that will be discussed in detail.*

A post is an entry made in the immutable leger system which I will refer
to as the forum going forward. A post can contain a creation by an
individual or user. An individual can be anyone outside the system,
while a user is an individual that is represented on the system through
an account.

## Example Photography

1.  A user posts a photograph of bird, with subject tag #photography,
    #nature photography

2.  A day later, the post receives 1 vote in both tags. the original
    poster receives 1 experience in #nature, and 1 experience in #nature
    photography

In this example, there was nothing more complex than the artistic
appreciation from one individual to another in the form of giving them
experience in photography tags.

## Example Debate

1.  A user makes a post about why they think a #political policy is bad
    and states their points

2.  Several user attempts to refute their points with their own points

3.  Other users support the original posters post with their own
    responses

4.  Users upvote and downvote the supporting posts and responses they
    think add value to the subject

In this example, there might not be a conclusion, or a right and wrong
side, but there is a clear discussion with consensus from multiple
parties through voting. In the end there is an information package
towards a specific subject that should provide a comprehensive dialogue
with supporting arguments and evidence. So that when it comes time to
make an actionable vote, this discussion can be referenced in the vote,
for all to see (more on actionable votes later).

## dFluid Representation

In this open style of discussion and debating, everyone has equal
opportunity to act in the role of the parliament or the senate. The more
value your discussion point creates in a debate, the more votes it will
receive, the more attention it will gain, and the poster will gain more
voting power through the experience they gain. An individual\'s post or
response might contain the reasoning needed for a vote to pass or not
pass.

A user gaining experience in a specific set of subjects can be said to
make them an expert in that field, and their voting power as a result is
much higher than the average individual. This in a sense can be thought
of as representation from a subject matter expert. At any point, their
negative actions which are deemed harmful to those subjects can result
in losing experience and no longer holding the same amount of voting
power, hence fluid representation.

## Identity-less Solutions

### Voting

Current voting solutions rely on centralized systems that issue, manage
and enforce identity documentation, papers or cards. These systems have
been exploited countless times historically. Since current identity
systems rely on centralized systems, open systems cannot rely on any
traditional identity systems; an open and decentralized system can never
depend on a centralized system, making it indirectly centralized.

Later in this document a method of identity voting will also be
integrated for local and membership voting, though I believe most global
voting will be done through identity-less voting.

### Accounts

With accounts having no identity by default, the focus of open systems
becomes purely creation. With no identity there is no bias associated
with identity, as a result users have an equal opportunity to all
aspects of the system.

It can be argued that the human condition will always contain a plethora
of biases, many of which we are not cognisant of at the time. While
these biases might have had some utility in the physical realm, in the
digital realm, they serve no purpose, and the focus should be on the
value created, not who created it.

Humanity working on improving their biases is out of the scope of this
document, and it is something that should continue to deserve effort.

# Initial Experience

Since the voting system is just a transfer of experience from one
individual onto another for the merits of their creation, experience
must initially be created with the system. I propose 2 methods for
initially creating experience:

A creator can earn experience even if their creation receives no votes,
as long as it did not receive any down votes or reports (more on reports
later). This is the first method for introducing experience into the
system or new subject when no other individual currently has any.

An individual may also earn experience by subscribing as an auditor. An
auditor has no power over any individual, they are simply able to earn
experience by assessing votes(more on this later). To become an auditor
experience is required and must be locked into auditing in specific
subject tags. E.g. When a creation is reported, auditors are selected at
random in order to vote with their locked experience if the creation has
broken any rules, e.g. plagiaristic.

# Actionable Voting & Subscriptions

Until this point we have looked at voting in order to assess value
creation. Voting is also used for decision making. E.g. when a change to
the rules is proposed, or a change to the software that these digital
open systems operate on must be approved democratically.

Actionable voting functions the same as assessment voting except that it
will have a passing condition which executes some predefined action.
These conditions and actions will be defined in smart contract: logic
that is stored in an immutable decentralized leger and executed on the
decentralized system.

Whereas assessment voting has no limit; users can vote as much as they
desire with no end limit of how many votes a creation can receive, an
actionable vote requires a limit in order to achieve a finite point that
defines consensus, in order to make a decision. These can be time based
limits, or vote count based limits. E.g. After some time, if there are
more votes than downvotes, a vote will pass, or if a vote count limit is
passed, the vote is passed. Both of these have their pros and cons and
are suitable for different situations.

Regardless of the voting limit type, actionable votes first require
criteria subjects to be selected. These are the subjects that users must
have experience in to vote if the actionable vote passes. The criteria
is typically defined by assessment vote. All voting criteria that gets
selected must have its own passing vote in order for the actionable vote
to pass. *Think of it as a panel of experts for each relevant subject
that must vote independently of every other subject panel in order for
an actionable vote to pass.* *E.g. if a vote requires both #art and
#tech, then two separate expert groups must both pass independently for
the actionable vote to pass. If one does not pass, the actionable vote
does not pass.*

Subscriptions are a vote count limit based method of voting, it allows
users to subscribe to a post with specific subject experience they lock
in. After subscribing, if an actionable vote is started, only users that
had previously subscribed are able to vote, and the actionable vote can
only pass when there are more than half of the votes to experience that
has been subscribed.

## Example Software Update

A programmer posts an improvement to some software with the tags
#programming and #smart contract. Other users upvote with other specific
subject tags. When the votes begin to settle down, and users have had a
chance to review and comment with no one stating any issues a user then
starts the vote by responding to the post with an actionable vote that
contains a smart contract which takes the software update and makes it
public. Any user is able to start the actionable vote at any time. Since
the post is an actionable vote, only users that have subscribed to the
original post (knowing it would eventually need a vote to integrate) are
able to vote. If there is a total of 1000 experience locked in to
#programming in then the actionable vote requires 501 votes to pass.
Once the #programming vote passes, the #smart contract vote still needs
to pass, as well as any other major tags that were voted on the original
post. Once all the major subject votes pass, the action is executed and
the software update is published.

An actionable vote status is kept hidden or encrypted until the vote
either passes or fails. This is to avoid any groupthink. Users are free
to reply with any concerns or ideas they have at any time.

If a vote fails, another vote can be started at any time, typically
after some concerns that caused it to fail are addressed.

Any experience locked into a subscription can be unlocked at any time,
and fully refunded. Voting with subscription does not consume the vote;
a user can vote on as many actionable votes as they like with the
experience they locked into the subscription. Voting with a subscription
does not earn any extra experience.
