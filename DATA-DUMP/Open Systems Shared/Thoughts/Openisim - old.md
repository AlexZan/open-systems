Openisim is an idea for global collaboration based on several
principles.

# Introduction to Principals

## Open: No Ownership

A system that is open can not be owned by any entity.

## Democratized

No entity can have any power over any individual in an open system. Any
action must be performed by community vote that everyone has equal
opportunity to.

## Decentralized

An open system can not be shut down or modified without a vote. It can
not be controlled by any entity.

An open system also can not have any dependency on a centralized system.
Being dependent on a centralized system means that an individual, group
or company can choose to shut down the centralized system, and thus the
dependent system will be non functional; The dependent system is
indirectly centralized by the centralized system.

## Transparent

All data is transparent. Any data going into an open system, is kept on
an immutable record, publically.

A critical point to understand is transparency does not mean lack of
privacy. All individual data is anonymous, enabling privacy.

# Decentralized Democracy

Because of the above principals requiring both decentralization and
democratization, decentralized democracy is a requirement.

Up until now, there has been no truly decentralized democracy solution.
Current democratic solutions rely on a centralized service indirectly
making it centralized. For example, to ensure that community members
each have one vote per person, they must prove their identity. Typically
this is done through the use of a government issued ID.

The issue with this is that the government body that manages and issues
IDs is centralized, and it can also currently be exploited. The
decentralized community that depends on the centralized service for
identification can not directly control improvements to reduce
exploitation for example.

Another issue with this is that government issued ID is not a global
solution, but only a national solution, and not all nations have the
same security standards of identity; A system for a global collaboration
can never depend on a nationally tied solution or service.

To summarize, a system that depends on a centralized system can not be
decentralized. In order for a system to be fully decentralized, all
decision making must be democratic.

## Why is a Decentralized Democracy needed?

What problems does Decentralized Democracy solve? The examples below are
not an exhaustive list.

### Decentralized Projects without Decentralized Development

Currently all projects that are decentralized, still have a centralized
development process and team. There is no way currently for a community
to vote fairly on a feature or direction of a project. The centralized
team makes these decisions. There is no decentralized way to vote on
accepting a new feature or bug fix completed by a community member, the
team must vote for it.

Decentralized Democracy will allow true decentralized development, where
the community completes features and bug fixes, but also votes on
accepting these features and bug fixes, as well as deciding what
features and bug fixes to prioritize.

### Crowd Funding and exploitation

Currently many crowd funded projects never make it to completion because
the project owners used the funds irresponsibility or there was fraud
involved. With Decentralized Democracy the community can vote to release
funds to the project owner as they prove they are progressing through
established project delivery dates. If a delivery does not meet the
requirements as voted by the community, the project owner must make the
community voted fixes and try again. Greatly decreasing the odds that a
project is fraudulent or the owner is negligent.

### Direct Democracy

Communities that use Decentralized Democracy no longer have to elect
representatives in order to vote on their behalf. Community members who
have experience and an interest in the subject being voted on can
directly affect the outcome instead of relying on a representative to
have their best interest.

### Global Collaboration

A system for crowd decision making and collaboration that is not tied to
any nation and is decoupled from any national control can provide a
system for true global collaboration.

## Achieving Decentralized Democracy: Open Democracy

There are several issues with our current form of representative
democracy: Not every voter understands the gravity of what they are
voting on, or has a comprehensive view of who they are voting on, yet
they get the same voting ability as an expert. This makes it very easy
to outvote experts, or those who are more likely to vote in a
communities best interest. Voters can be privately influenced to change
their vote, even though that vote might unknowingly, not be in
everyone\'s best interest. As well, representatives might vote with self
interest instead of community interest. Open Democracy provides fluid
experts which behave like representatives, without a set term electoral
process. The benefit of this is that people can just focus on providing
real value, and not have to spend energy on the set term electoral
process. Whether this is being voted in as an admin, into a board, or
into public office.

Identity plays the largest role in our current ideas of democracy, since
democracy has always been 1 vote per member. E.g member of a
country(citizen), member of club, member of board, etc. Identity can be
exploited, therefore voters and votes can be faked. This has happened
many times historically and voter fraud continues to be an ongoing
issue.

The solution is to not depend on membership and therefore identity.
Voting power will not be tied to identity. Members will no longer get
one vote by default. Instead voting power will depend on value created
through interactions, both local and global. This has many positive
consequences that will be outlined.

## Global interaction

Users who create value are rewarded with experience, this is the core
idea of global interaction.

Users can create value by posting content (artwork, idea, solution,
proof of work, etc) to a forum that uses open democracy features. Other
users can vote on the content they posted, as an expression of assessed
value, which translates into the experience points the original poster
earns. Users can create value in any type of subject, independent of
location or group, and are rewarded with experience points. Experience
points is the value of voting power.

E.g. I post a landscape photo and tag it with #photography. Over several
days, I receive 100 upvotes in #photography. I now have 100 #photography
experience points.

I contribute to a solution to a geopolitical problem and receive 10,000
votes in #politics, #policy, #geo politics, etc.

Only tags can be upvoted or downvoted and a user must have experience in
a tag in order to vote. Voting on a tag consumes that experience. E.g.
If I upvote a post's tag with 1 experience, I lose 1 experience. Users
can recover their consumed experience.

## Local interaction

A user that is new to a group or a location should have less influence
over decisions as a more seasoned user.

A neighbour, customer, club member, etc, can earn interaction points by
interacting with another member or local institution. Local interactions
can prove interaction with a group, general location (anonymous). This
data is saved to a decentralized leger anonymously. By tracking
interactions and time span with a group or a general location, a user
can have more influence over decisions in that group or locality than a
newer user or less involved user.

E.g. A local community must vote on whether a park gets an upgrade or
not. A user who is on the other side of the world can see the post, and
join in on the discussion, but because the decision will have no impact
on them, they should have no impact on the decision. Because the user
who is not local has no local interaction points, they can have no
effect on the decision.

## Decentralized Democracy Introductory Summary

Open Democracy is a decentralized democracy based on open principals,
that utilizes an identity-less solution with local and global
interaction tracking.

The amount of value someone creates in a world or system, directly
translates to their experience, and thus their voting power; A proven
expert should have more voting power in the subject they are an expert
in.

A person should have more impact over a local decision, the more that
decision impacts them; A person's local impact is proportional to a
person\'s local interaction.

## Requirements

Open Democracy requires a place where users can post about the value
they created; a forum. A system which can store immutable user
experience and post voting data. Local interactions require a system
which can store immutable user interaction and voting data.

## Technical

A blockchain based system is the ideal solution. Storing critical data
on the blockchain such as vote actions, counts, experience and
interactions, while storing non critical data such as post content,
interaction metadata, etc, and storing the hash of the non critical data
with the appropriate critical data in the blockchain.

## Voting Power

This section will begin to describe Decentralized Democracy through
examples that face technical challenges, but it is not exclusive to
technical challenges and can be used for any type of assessment and
decision making as will be covered later in the document.

All users start with 0 voting power when they first begin using an open
system; they are not able to vote until they acquire some voting power.
To acquire voting power, a user must first contribute some value to a
community. One example of contributing value is through an open
discussion forum.

An open forum is similar to a standard discussion forum, but users
upvote and downvote subject tags on posts, instead of the post itself.
E.g the tag #photography can be voted, not the post itself.

## Earning Experience

The best way to start earning experience points is by creating a post.

For example, You have an idea for an app. You post your app idea and tag
it with #app #idea. After one day, if no one believes it is a bad idea
and does not down vote the post, you are awarded 1 experience point in
both #app and #idea. **Experience is earned in subjects, even if no one
upvotes, as long as no one downvotes.**

If users upvote your idea in #app and #concept after one day, on top of
the 1 experience you earn by default, you also earn all the votes made
towards your post. E.g. there were 10 votes in #app, and 5 votes in
#concept. After a day you earn 11 votes in #app and 6 votes in #concept.
**Votes received are turned into experience points.**

A user upvotes your app idea with a new tag, for example if it is a
rideshare app idea, but you didn\'t initially tag it with #rideshare, a
user might add #rideshare as a new tag and give you 1 experience in that
subject. **Any subject can be upvoted or downvoted**

Essentially you are contributing value to the community just by posting
an idea, but you could also be a programmer submitting your code for
review as a post, earning points in #programming and #app. You could be
an artist posting your #painting. You could be a #mechanic posting a
#DIY video. **Any content can contribute value.**

[If a user just created an account, they can not upvote #app, since they
do not have any experience in #app. **You must have experience in order
to vote.**]{.mark}

When a user votes on a post\'s tag, the experience they voted with is
consumed. E.g. you upvote #app, and you lose 1 experience from your
total experience point. Users will then spend their experience only on
posts that they think created true value. **Voting power is consumed.**

When a user votes on a tag, and the vote count doubles from the point at
which they voted, they will also earn experience. E.g. User x has 1
experience point in #app and upvotes #app when it had 0 votes, taking it
to 1 vote. Another user upvotes #app and it is now 2 votes, causing user
x to earn back 1 experience point in #app. This incentives users to
discover valuable posts early. **Experience points can be recovered by
voting early.**

If you find a post that you think has a great deal of potential, you may
upvote it for as many experience points as you have. E.g. You can upvote
100 points in #app, thinking that this is a great idea, and if the post
will reach a high number of votes, it will allow you to earn back 200
experience points as an investment. **You can upvote with multiple
points.**

If a user posts something ignorant, or something that would cause a
decrease in value in some subject, users can downvote the post in those
tags. E.g. A user makes a post about making money by inconveniencing
other people, this user could get downvotes in #ethics. **Users can lose
experience by being downvoted.**

A tag can only receive as many downvotes as the total amount of upvotes
in that tag -1. E.g. this post has 0 upvotes in #ethics, a user can
downvote it just one time down to -1, but if another user disagrees and
upvotes #ethics, it has one upvote, and one downvote, for a total of 0
points. At this point users could downvote the tag two more times taking
it down to -2. **Downvoting has a limit.**

## Actionable Voting

Until this point we have covered upvoting and downvoting in order to
assess the value of something (a post, an accomplishment, some work,
etc). Users can upvote indefinitely with no end; with no vote count
threshold that triggers an event. The higher the vote count gets, the
more value it represents.

Some community collaborations require decisions to be made and actions
to be taken. An example of that is something called a bounty. A bounty
is a goal that anyone can attempt to achieve. A bounty is posted like
any other type of content, but users are able to fund the bounty with
crypto (crypto currency). As the bounty fund grows larger and larger,
users might be more motivated to attempt to achieve it. A user can
respond to the bounty with a submission. If a day passes and the
submission has double or more of the upvotes than the downvotes, the
submission is accepted and the funds are delivered to the user who made
the submission and won the bounty.

More details on the entire funding process and funding application will
be covered later in this document.

### Technical 

Voting in a bounty has an end goal, to award a community voted winner
with the fund. As a result, it has a smart contract that is instantiated
when the bounty is created. This smart contract will activate when a
submission is made by a user. The smart contract will be responsible for
the action of delivering the funds when the vote passing condition is
met.

This is just one example of an actionable vote, but there are many more,
and anyone can develop their own for use in the system.

## Criteria of Actionable Votes

Actionable votes allow a smart contract to execute. The process of a
vote passing requires voting criteria to be selected before the final
vote. For example, If a bounty is created in order to create a new app
feature, as the bounty's funding is growing, users can also start voting
which tags they think will be required. If the feature is a smart
contract feature, users can suggest #smart contract, #app development,
#programming, etc; any subject that the decision will need to be judged
on. By the time the first submission of the bounty is made, and/or more
than 24 hours since the bounty was made, the voting criteria must be
selected.

I propose the following equation in order to determine the criteria vote
count lower limit (the point at which subjects will not count as
criteria): The average of the positive lower median.

### Use Case

1.  Bounty made with smart contract development goal

2.  Users suggest the following tags: #smart contract, #app development,
    #programming, #solidity, #app, #finance, #funny.

3.  Several days later the first programmer submits their code for
    review as a submission. By this time #smart contract had 100 votes,
    #app development had 200 votes, #programming had 1000 votes,
    solidity had 50 votes, #app had 5 votes, #finance had 1 vote,
    #design had 2 votes, and #funny had -2 votes.

4.  The vote criteria is determined by getting the average of the upper
    median:

> (2+1)/2 = 3/2= 1.5

5.  Since #funny was a negative vote, it never made it as a criteria
    subject. Since #finance was only 1 vote, and the cut off was 1.5, it
    never made it as a subject criteria. #design made it since it had 2
    votes which was greater than the 1.5 vote cutoff.

6.  Users begin to vote each criteria which they have experience in.

7.  24 hours later, all the votes are in the positive, the submission
    passes and the bounty fund is awarded to the programmer.

These criteria selection votes are what drive all actionable votes, no
matter if it is a bounty, goal, or any other type of smart contract.

## Open Democracy Summary

Users will no longer get one vote by default, instead users get voting
power in proportion to the value they have created, represented by
experience. This in a sense allows for fluid representation with a fluid
electoral process. Those who have created value for the rest, represent
the interest of the rest in the specific subjects that describe the
value they created. Value is assessed by the community through a
democratic process.

# Funding

Discussions and decisions can be made on a forum, but actionable items
often need funding in order to be realised. E.g. An idea can be thought
up on a forum, but to create that idea, it typically needs funding.

The systems such as the forum which Openisim requires to function will
be maintained and further developed using open principals. E.g. If a
user finds a problem with the software or a rule, or thinks of a better
idea, they can first discuss it on the forum, then If there is interest
in it, a fund can then be started in order to fund its development.

There are several types of funds, such as; Bounties, Goals and Projects.

## Bounties

A bounty is a fund with the reward being open to anyone that proves they
were first to meet the bounty criteria.

E.g. There is a bug with a new feature in an open system, a bounty is
created to fix it, and users begin to donate to the bounty over time.
The bounty reaches an amount that becomes very attractive to developers,
they now race to develop and submit the fix as a reply to the bounty
post. When users vote that it has in fact fixed the bug, the bounty is
awarded or held for some time before it is awarded.

## Goals

A goal is a fund with the reward being reserved for the original poster
or a predetermined account. This is different from a bounty, where any
user can receive the bounty fund.

E.g. A user promises to create something if a specific funding goal is
reached.

Goals are similar to today\'s crowdfunding funding goals.

## Projects

A project is a set of goals. Goals can have predetermined criteria that
the owner specifies, beyond just the funding criteria, such as in the
standard goal mentioned earlier.

E.g. In a project to design an electric scooter the first goal is to
create concept art for the scooter design. It has a funding requirement
of \$5,000 crypto equivalent. The user then posts proof of goal
completion; the scooter concept art. The community votes on whether they
accept or reject the submitted proof of completion. If the vote passes,
the \$5,000 crypto fund is released to the user. This allows the user to
now begin work on the next goal, unlocking the funding of each goal as
they progress through the project.

By having a crowdfunded project broken down into several goals that
unlock funding through a democractic process solves some of the most
common issues with current crowd funding by democratizing the entire
crowd funding process through the open principles, and factoring in
trust into funding progression

# Conflict Resolution and Claims

The rules, contracts and software in open systems will always be
evolving, as such there will be constant problems that must be
identified and overcome. There might also be conflict between multiple
entities that must also be identified and overcome.

A claims system is thus required that meets all of the open principals.
A system and process which allows any user to post a claim with a
structure for supporting evidence. Users can then respond to the claim
or to each item of supporting evidence with their own supporting
evidence or a comment.

Users can then propose resolutions to the identified conflict and an
eventual resolution can be voted on. If for some reason the open claims
process fails and local legal institutions must be pursued, the
information from the claims post can be used in the local case.

This process can be used beyond just the immediate resolution process
but to help understand and avoid future conflicts; this is where
proposed theories are valuable; they try to make sense of the conflict;
they try to find patterns in similar conflicts, creating a life cycle of
community reflection and improvement across all open systems.

## Encryption and Security

### Private Data and Encryption

Encrypted data is permitted as long as it follows all open principles.
If data is encrypted, it must be encrypted for all, with no one having
the ability to decrypt the data. Data can only be encrypted/decrypted by
vote or by contract.

In some cases data can be decrypted for a specific user, entity or set
of users, but this action must be a community vote.

E.g. A security camera is recording data to the cloud and it is encoded
so that no one can view the recorded video. If the video needs to serve
as evidence in a claim, local police case or local trial then a request
can be made to provide access to a specific user(s). This request must
then be voted on, if it passes, only those users will be able to view
the video for x amount of time.
