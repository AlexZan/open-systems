It was spring of 2021, I decided to buy some land to build my dream
workshop and house on since there wasn't much else to do with the covid
pandemic inching towards an endemic. After some shopping around, and
dealing with selling my property, I took possession in August. Just
after mid august I received my first vaccine; Phizer. I was in the ER in
the first week with pain in both arms, pins and needles and hot and cold
sensations, I was in the ER again as it progressed into my feet, and
then eventually into my trunk. It has now been about 3 months since my
first ER visit and the neurologist appointment I was booked for, in
order to diagnose my neuropathy symptoms has never reached out to me
even though I called several times and left voicemails. I have been to
several other walk-in doctors, who referred me to other specialists,
none of which I am able to get appointments with or get my calls
returned.

I did not get my vaccine when it first became available because I was
not legible; you had to be over 40 in Oakville, Ontario in order to get
it. I tried going to other cities but they checked my address. Most
people were not directly saying it, but I was already getting labeled an
antivaxer because I did not have my vaccine yet. Friends and family in
the majority of Toronto area that had access to their shots even 6
months before me, could not understand why I didn\'t have it yet. I was
already seeing the early effects of social polarization, and I was
shocked to see it developing in people that were very close to me.

In the summer when they opened up vaccinations to under 40 in Oakville,
the cases were so low (under 100 a day in the province of Ontario) and
dropping gradually, that I decided to only take it if the cases begin to
rise again. I was a little worried with such a short approval phase for
the vaccine, and hearing reports of myocarditis and blot clotting issues
with getting it when the cases were low and dropping, it was a personal
risk management decision as I have had heart arrhythmia on several
occasions in the past, and I have a genetic blood clot condition. The
social pressure to get jabbed started to rise. Friends and family who
were not aware of my choices for not getting the vaccine were telling me
daily to just get jabbed, not think about it.

It started to become clear that you either were part of the vaccinated
group, or not, reasons did not seem to matter, if you had valid concerns
with covid vaccines, you were labeled an antivaxer regardless, or at
least that\'s how it began to feel.

It was a very lonely time for me, since I was not going out, I was
social-distancing, isolating, and lived alone.

It was August and I was in my planning phase since it had just been a
couple weeks since I just took possession of my new land. All my things
were in temporary storage. The covid cases began to rise, I decided it
was time to get the jab as my social duty.

I went to a local clinic, got the injection and was out 30 minutes
later. I was surprised two days later how I had no pain on the arm I got
injected in since I heard that from so many people, a few days after
that I started to get strange sensations and pain that continued to
escalate each day. I started having sleepless nights because of the
constant radiating pain in my arms and hands. Touching the sheets and
pillow felt strange but I could not explain it. After a couple nights of
no sleep I drove to the ER. I was seen very quickly, and explained my
symptoms. I was told it should be gone in a few weeks and prescriptions
for gabapentin and physiotherapy were given to me. I was given no
diagnosis, nothing was said about the vaccine. I asked why I was not
given any tests to diagnose me, the doctor replied that I am not severe
enough and it is not urgent. I was dismissed from the hospital. I tried
to take gabapentin over several days, but it made me quite high and did
not do anything for my symptoms.

The pain continued to increase, and started to make its way to my legs
and feet. I tried driving to several walk-in clinics, but I was rejected
at all of them since none of them were doing in person visits at the
time, and the waiting list for an appointment was days to weeks. The
pain became so bad that I could not walk. I went to the ER a second
time. I was in distress. I was again seen very quickly, there was no
lineup to get into the ER. This time they ran a blood test, nothing came
up. I was told I need to see a neurologist to diagnose this but that
none are available for me today since I am not urgent. I was told it
would need to affect my motor nerves for it to be considered more
urgent, but that a neurologist appointment was made for me. I expressed
concerns with permanent damage if it\'s not treated early but was told
there is nothing they can do.

When the idea of being rational can be irrational if compassion is
disregarded.
