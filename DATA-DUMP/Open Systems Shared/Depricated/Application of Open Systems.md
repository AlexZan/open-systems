The initial application of Open Systems, also referred to as Open World,
is a set of systems and applications that are designed, communicate and
operate based on a set of defined Open Systems Principles. Open World is
a system for collaboration of all things. Open World will also serve as
the foundation for other Open Systems.

Link to document: [[Open Systems
Principles]{.underline}](https://docs.google.com/document/u/0/d/12kuFI0-LslYEHr20CSyu0-6PYzH8fDbUX247YxRntz0/edit)

Open World has a set of 3 core systems

1.  Forum

2.  Funding

3.  Claims

These systems are tech agnostic. Meaning they can be built and operated
with any tech as long as they meet the criteria for Open Systems. Open
Systems and Open World is not a tech solution, though it requires some
tech.

# Forum

The forum (Based on traditional forum concepts) is where content is
posted and discussions are had. All content; ideas, images, videos,
articles, etc, are posted in the forum. Discussions can then occur by
replying to the content posted.

## Voting

Traditional digital forums might allow users to like, or upvote a post;
with Open Forum, this is where the differences begin.

In Open Forum you can upvote or downvote only subject tags/hashtags of a
post, not the post itself. E.g. If someone posts a grand canyon photo
they took a user might upvote a #grand canyon tag, another user could
upvote #photography, and yet another might upvote #landscape
photography. This is to enable universal democracy as defined in
[[Universal Democracy and Open
Systems]{.underline}](https://docs.google.com/document/u/0/d/1WqUw1YXJoIW1yStiPJsvw_s6qmLizn8dn3ZwUFU73KE/edit).

## Experience

The original poster gains experience equal to the amount of votes they
receive in each tag. E.g. the user will get 10 experience in
#photography because there were 10 votes in photography from other
users. We will see how experience turns into merritt which affects
voting power later on.

## Voting as an Investment

A user can only vote if they themselves have experience in that subject.
When they vote on a subject, they are consuming the experience points
they currently have in order to cast the vote. In some cases the user
who voted will get back their experience in that subject. As you can
see, voting can be an investment (most interactions with the system are
investments; monetary or experiential).

## Multiple Votes

A user can vote as many times as they like, as long as they have
experience points to do so. In fact a user can vote however many votes
they want in a single vote. E.g You vote 10 votes on #photography
because you had 10 experience in #photography. After voting you have 0
experience in #photography and must wait for the vote to double in count
in order to double the experience points you invested.

This Multiple votes feature is based on the open systems principles and
specifically the "No Individual Voting Power" derived principle. Another
benefit to this is that there is no way to exploit voting through
identification fraud, since voting is not tied to any type of
identification, but instead only to earned merit.

## Gaining Experience

There are many things a user can do to gain experience. The first is
they can post original content. Depending on how well the content is
received, the original poster could earn a great deal of experience or
lose experience. Experience gained or lost should be directly
proportional to the value provided to the community. E.g. A great photo
was taken and posted to #photography, the #photography community
appreciated the great work and rewarded the user with votes, and thus
experience, as well as donations or sponsorship (more on that later). As
we can see tags are subjects and eventually communities form around
those subjects.

Just like gaining experience, a user can also lose experience from
downvotes up to a certain limit.

## Accounts

A new user can start using the forum right away without any setup: A
user without an account can still post content to the forum, this
content will be posted as anonymous.

In order to vote and gain experience, you must create an account. This
is a simple process of storing credentials that protect the key to your
account. This key must be kept secure at all times, just like your house
keys or any key to a store of value such as a safety deposit box at a
bank.

## Open Posts

Thus far we have talked about posts where the content that was posted is
owned by the original poster. The data in them can not be modified by
the community, only by the original poster.

E.g. You post an idea for a new app, only you can delete the post or
modify it, no one else can: You own the content.

If we wanted to make a post that everyone could contribute to, such as
the concept of a "wiki", we could create an open post.

E.g. You post about the history of Egypt. Anyone can make edit
suggestions which the community can vote in. No user including the
original poster can delete or modify the post.

## Doubling Experience

A user will double the amount of experience when the vote count doubles
from the point at which they voted. E.g. you vote one #photography
upvote on a post when it had 20 votes, a few hours later the post
reached 40 votes and you now receive two (double) #photograph experience
points.

## Down Votes

If someone posted something inaccurate or untrue in a subject, users may
downvote it. E.g. a user posts about a flat earth theory, you might
downvote #science on their post. This will cause their account to to
have a -1 in #science. Though flat earthers might upvote #flatearth and
they will gain experience in that subject.

If someone posts an unappealing photo to #photography, a user will
probably not downvote #photography because 1) it is far more subjective
to assess general art appeal and 2) because the odds of doubling your
experience are far less likely since many people will not vote due to
point 1. An unappealing photo might have few #photography votes or none,
but they are likely not to have negative votes.

Therefore negative votes are more likely to be used when assessing more
objective truths; things that can be proven through reason or are
quantifiable.

# Funding

Thus far we have talked about posting content in exchange for
experience. This is essentially working with sweat equity, as that
experience can lead to improving your odds of earning a profit, though
experience can never directly be exchanged for monetary value as it
would violate the principles of Universal Democracy (more on that
later).

## Accounts

By default each account is also able to store crypto currency (crypto).
This does not require any setup or any deposits. It is just an optional
place to store and receive crypto.

## Donations

A user can donate to a post, and the original poster will receive the
donations in their account. Donating to a post will also show the
donation publicly on the post itself, and the total amount that has been
donated to the post. E.g. A remarkable photo of the grand canyon has
received 10 donations totalling \$200 dollars equivalent in crypto.

#### Funds

Users can create a funding post. This creates crypto storage in the post
with certain rules. There are different type funds with different rules,
the most common are bounties and goals.

If a user donates to a post that has a fund, the donation will go to the
fund instead of the original poster directly.

## Bounties

A bounty is a fund with the reward being open to anyone that proves they
were first to meet the bounty criteria.

E.g. There is a bug with a new feature in an open system, a bounty is
created to fix it, and users begin to donate to the bounty over time.
The bounty reaches an amount that becomes very attractive to developers,
they now race to develop and submit the fix as a reply to the bounty
post. When users vote that it has in fact fixed the bug, the bounty is
awarded or held for some time before it is awarded.

## Goals

A goal is a fund with the reward being reserved for the original poster
or a predetermined account.

E.g. An individual is shown as a victim in a video that goes viral. A
user sets up an account on their behalf and a fund goal post is made as
a reply to the original video post that went viral. It quickly gets
voted to the top and users begin to make donations to support the
victim. The victim is then contacted and asked to take control of the
account and verify (more on this later). Users see that the victim has
verified they have control of the account that will receive the goal
fund once it is reached. The goal fund is reached and the funds are
released.

## Funding

In order to donate to someone, you simply deposit crypto from an
existing crypto exchange or address you have crypto into, to the address
of the user account you wish to donate to. If you are not familiar with
crypto exchanges, wallets and addresses, this will be described later.
Essentially a crypto exchange is a service that allows you to exchange
one currency for another, and so you can take dollars, and purchase
crypto coin, then use that to donate or invest in Open Funds. The
important note here is that Open World can utilize existing
decentralized infrastructure in order to make funding work and does not
require its own.

You can also transfer crypto into your own forum account, that way you
can donate directly from within the forum application. This is optional.

## Projects

A project is a set of goals. Goals can have predetermined criteria that
the owner specifies, beyond just the funding criteria, such as in the
standard goal mentioned earlier.

E.g. In a project to design an electric scooter the first goal can be to
create concept art for the scooter design. It has a funding requirement
of \$5,000 crypto equivalent. When the funding goal has been met, the
user posts proof of goal completion. The community votes on whether they
accept or reject the submitted proof of completion. If the vote passes,
the \$5,000 crypto fund is released to the user. This allows the user to
now begin work on the next goal, unlocking the funding of each goal as
they progress through the project.

By having a crowdfunded project broken down into several goals that
unlock funding through a democractic process solves some of the most
common issues with current crowd funding by democratizing the entire
crowd funding process through the open principles, and factoring in
trust into funding progression

## Trust

Projects can be set up in many ways with many options. Some options will
create more trust, some will create less trust. One of these options is
to allow the project to become Open if it meets failure criteria. E.g.
If a project that was funded fully, but the owner loses interest half
way through and disappears, the community can vote the project as a
failure, this will make the project open; a crowd sourced project. The
original owner will automatically relinquish all legal rights by
contract, to anything that was created through the project funding thus
far.

In this example, users are more likely to fund this project because they
trust that if the original owner fails, the project will continue.

There are many more examples of how project trust can be increased (more
on this later).

One last example is that owners can gradually increment the funding
requirement for each goal, so that trust grows proportionally with the
funding being unlocked, as opposed to all or a large amount of funding
being unlocked initially.

## Tokens

In traditional crowdfunding, the user has the option of pre-purchasing a
product or service at a discount. The earlier the pre purchase, the more
of a discount received. Projects in Open Systems also have the option
for this with tokens. A token can be purchased at an established value
by the owner, and once the product or service is ready, they can
exchange the token for the product or service.

Another benefit to using tokens is that if local laws permit, the owner
can allow the token to be transferable and thus tradable on exchanges.
This is essentially like purchasing stock in a company early on such as
in an IPO, in order to make a profit off your speculation of increase in
value.

This will open the door to projects receiving much more diverse funding
from investors that are not interested in the product or service but
only want to fund the project to make a profit.

The project owner will be liable for any legal consequences and
responsibilities if tokens are not allowed to be transferable in their
local laws. This type of project should be reserved for more experienced
or expert project owners. Investors might not have trust in an
inexperienced owner trying to launch this type of project.

# Claims

When there is a bad actor a claim must be made so that either the system
can evolve to disable that type of bad actor in the future, and or for
some accountability and retribution.

Claims can lead to the following:

1.  Permanent history on the account of the claim and its status

2.  Funding that was held to be returned

3.  Assets that were funded through Open Funding to have their ownership
    transferred to the community or made open source.

4.  Local jurisdiction charges filed with evidence gathered in claim

E.g. A user creates a token project, makes a great deal of promises in
each goal, and begins to deliver fraudulent evidence as proof of
completing goals, such as creating fake images, fake videos, showing
falsified financial documents. Some users begin to find suspicious
activity and create a bounty for showing irrefutable evidence that the
project is a fraud. Other users that are also suspicious begin to donate
to the bounty. Users begin to investigate and they are able to prove the
content is false. Several claims are posted with evidence showing proof
of fraud. The claims are voted and passed. The owner as a result loses
experience in the tags that were voted. The owner also has the claims on
their permanent account history. Furthermore the local authorities are
contacted and the claims and evidence is used to lay down charges on the
owner.

Users vote not continue with the project as an open project and all the
outstanding funds are returned to the users. Because the fraud was
caught in just the initial goals, before the large funds were available
in the later goals, the majority of the funds were returned. The
remainder could be recovered from local legal means with the assistance
of further bounties or claims.

Although it is extremely simple to create a new account if you received
bad history from claims on your existing account, it is not simple to
develop experience and trust on the new account. A great deal of value
is lost when an experienced account gets a bad history, and a decision
should be made if it is better to seek redemption and keep the existing
experience, or if it is better to start from zero again. Redemption can
always be appreciated by the community if it teaches a valuable lesson.
A bad history followed by a long good history might even be more
trustworthy than a good but shorter history on an account

# Accounts

As we can imagine by now, building an expert account takes a great
amount of effort and time. Therefore expertise can be leveraged against
trust, just like something of monetary collateral value. E.g. The
community is more likely to fund a goal created by an expert account,
then a new account because there is value for the user of the account to
lose if they do not meet the goal criteria by the deadline.

## Multiple Accounts

A user can create as many accounts as they like. The only advantage they
can gain from doing so is that they can create a disconnect between the
subjects they participate with the different accounts in. e.g. They
might have a public account which they verified and participate in
political subjects, and another private account where they might debate
more controversial subjects.

There is no advantage to voting by creating multiple accounts since
voting power is achieved through merit.

# Policies

All policies must be voted into implementation. Though anyone can create
or modify the policy and submit it for vote, in order to be implemented
into the system. A policy is simply a rule written in human readable
language and translated into computer code. The policies must be
contained and executed by open systems; meaning all the open principles
must apply. A solution such as a blockchain smart contract is ideal.,

An example would be the core policy which defines a vote as a success;
whenever the amount of votes double from the initial vote. This can be
changed to three times, or 2.5 times the initial amount through
community vote.

Every single behaviour of every single open system is performed through
this type of democratized policy.

# Advanced Topics

## Zero Day Experience

If users vote with experience, how do you gain experience in a tag no
one has experience in yet, such as in the first day of the start of the
system.

A user makes a post and they set some initial tag(s) upon posting. If
the user does not have any experience in the tag(s) they set in their
post, then after one day, they gain one experience in the tag(s). If
within that day they receive only a down vote, and have less than 0
votes, the experience is no longer given to them.

## Self Evolving

The systems that allow for projects to be community developed, funded
and maintained also works on itself. Open Forum and any other Open
System will be constantly evolving through a cycle of:
feedback-\>discussion-\>feature request-\>funding-\>development. All the
policies that govern the systems will also follow the same cycle of
constant improvement

# Conclusion

Open world is a set of open source, decentralized, democratized systems
working together through a set of common principles.

A core open system is a system which all other open systems rely on to
function. E.g open health requires open forums, but open forums does not
require open health. These features introduced above and the principals
introduced in the open systems principles document set the groundwork
for all other open systems to come.

Open world and any of its systems have no owner or any goals for
monetization, ever. The open world is for everyone, anywhere to freely
collaborate, share the joy of today through, and make tomorrow better
with.
