## Game Theory

This is a game known as the prisoner\'s dilemma in Game Theory.
Prisoner\'s Dilemma is probably best explained through an online video,
but a very basic explanation is as follows: that if someone seeks to
profit from the loss of another (even if unethical), it is in
everyone\'s best interest to be first to profit. It is the expected
behavior and most optimal social strategy. The solution to repeating
prisoner\'s dilemma is tit for tat.

nearly impossible to function. Without tit for tat functioning
consistently in populated areas or online, the best strategy is
selfishness, and it would be foolish to do otherwise. This needs to
change.

## Competition and Deception

Would capitalism fully function without deception? Does the anti
competition behavior resulting from competition get solved by systems of
anti deception? If the largest company buys their only competitor, and
does so without any deceptive tactics. The company will then own that
market, and have the greatest control over margins, and push them in
their favor and away from the favor of consumers. This will create a
very attractive market segment for entry from competition. This creates
a medusa effect. This makes the buyout unattractive, maintaining
competition, and a balance between consumer and company, supply and
demand. With deception, a company could use hostile techniques such as
pricing out a competitor from entry. New companies could then never
enter that market and the monopoly is maintained.

Is pricing out competition is a deceptive practice? The idea is that the
monopoly sells things below cost, losing money up front, while not
allowing the competitor to earn a profit. The hope is that the new
competitor will dry up funds before the monopoly does, which usually has
a lot more equity, as well as other income sources it can draw from. The
monopoly could be open about this tactic, and consumers would still
purchase the things at a lower cost, regardless if it hurts them in the
long run. So then how is this deceptive? Maybe because it an investment
on the lack of knowledge of the average consumer, to not know, that
buying cheap in the short run will hurt them in the long run.

Would majority of consumers knowing that buying cheap in the short term
cause higher prices long term stop the consumer from buying from the
monopoly? Its a prisoners dilemma, whoever purchases first wins the
game, so no. So then it does not appear to be deception. Is Capitalism
then always bound to fail? Does capitalism always result in anti
competition?

Does this apply to the idea marketplace? Competing for goal completion,
