# Case Studies

### IBackPack

Kickstarter Project in 2015, raise \$700k and project was dissolved with
a bogus reason. Exit Scam. How could have OpenFunder helped?

Since the owners of the project would probably not have had any success
stories on OFer, Their project would probably not have received much
attention until they had several detailed Goals listed out.

Since the prototype was designed already, the next goal would have been
to setup manufacturing and shipping logistics.

#### Possible Goals with OFer

1.  setup manufacturing logistics

    a.  talk with several companies comparing production costs. \$10k

2.  setup shipping logistics

    a.  talk with several shipping companies comparing costs. \$5k

3.  Testing phase.

    a.  Manufacture and ship out 100 units to early buy ins. Create
        review form for testers. Majority of testers to fill out form
        before deadline. \$10k

4.  Full Production

    a.  Manufacture and ship out 1000 units at a time. 1000 x \$120.

    b.  Buyers would need to vote they received their order and the goal
        is complete.

Every goal has a funding hold time, e.g. a month, and so if a goal is
not completed before then, funds are not delivered, and customers can
eventually get refunded.

### Elio Motors Scooter

"Elio Motors launched a crowdfunding campaign promoting a new 3-wheeled
electric vehicle that was supposed to hit the markets in 2014 with
incredible fuel efficiency. The company was certainly good at one thing:
Raising money from casual investors, to the tune of \$17 million for its
first crowdfunding round, including over 65,000 that reserved a model
ahead of time. Elio even hit the road with a conference on how to
properly raise money via crowdfunding.

Then ... well, nothing happened. Elio Motors burned through its
investment money in only a few short months, primarily on suspicious
soft expenses easily summed up as "paychecks." The supposed released
date has been pushed back for years, Elio has failed to get any
additional loans for production, and it is now obvious there will never
be an Elio scooter. The company is currently selling its small amount of
manufacturing equipment just to keep the lights on, and is expected to
collapse entirely any time now."

This seems to be a mix of mis management with gradual exit scam

### Skarp Laser Razor

"The Skarp Laser Razor was supposedly going to revolutionize shaving by
using a laser to remove hair. Apparently, human hair contains a
chromophore (a particle that can absorb certain wavelengths of light)
that allows follicles to be cut when hit with a particular wavelength of
light.

The company behind the razor, Skarp Technologies, claimed to have a
working prototype. However, the video on the project's page didn't
exactly garner confidence from skeptics. Sure --- Skarp's technology
might able to snip off a few hairs, but it wouldn't be anywhere near as
effective as a regular ol' razor.

Kickstarter eventually stepped in after more than 20,000 backers raised
more than \$4 million in funding. An email sent to backers soon reported
that the company did not actually have a working prototype, and the
company was in violation of a rule that requires working prototypes of
physical products that are offered as rewards.

Shortly thereafter, the project quickly moved itself over to IndieGoGo,
where it raised close to \$500,000. Skarp is still coming out with
periodic updates about...uh, lavish, empty meeting rooms and more
pseudo-science about fine-tuning their fibers."

This seems to be 95% exit scam and 5% unfeasible tech and mis-management

#### Possible Goals with Ofer

The same Goals as previous cases would apply, but since the video of the
proof of concept would not be voted on, it would have failed right there
and all funds returned.

### Cataldo's Cancer

"One of the most infamous crowdfunding cancer stories comes from
Alabama, where Jennifer Flynn Cataldo, 37, set up GoFundMe campaigns to
help pay for medical bills associated with her cancer. She got donations
of more than \$38,000, before being quickly convicted of fraud. Her
assets are now being seized to pay back donors.

While this story had a happy ending, cancer scams are incredibly common.
In fact, it's a good policy to never trust a crowdfunding campaign for
any kind of medical bills or any personal problems whatsoever. It's far
too difficult to verify these costs, and much too easy to get distracted
by the emotional stories."

This is a scam, one that if it had a solution, could help many people
who could actually use that money. This one will be tricky, but lets
try.

#### Possible Goals with Ofer

1.  Need 20,000 to cover medical bills. With the following requirements:

    a.  Doctor signed note of diagnosis

    b.  Doctor signed tests and results proving diagnosis

    c.  Hospital Bill with detail breakdown

    d.  Exchange to convert to local currency and to wire money directly
        to hospital, hospital to verify transaction received by 15
        random supporters. Funds never touch the hands of any
        individual, but pay bill directly.
