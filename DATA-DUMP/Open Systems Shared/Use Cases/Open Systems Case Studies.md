# Communities

A community has members, and members can have stake in the community
assets.

## Maker Space

### Member Only

A makerspace has members, only these members should be able to vote on
items that only affect the members or the member community.

"What tool should we get next?"

It makes no sense that anyone outside the community that would get to
use the tool, or that is paying towards it.

### Voting with Value

Maybe this should not be a voting decision but a price decision? For
example, Users can post their desires, I want a Laser CNC, I want a
plasma cutter, etc. And users can pledge money towards those funds. When
a fund gets large enough where a user thinks they can make profit by
buying and selling the item, they will do so. In this case it is much
more organic since there is no vote needed to manage the communities
funds.

Maybe all fund related votes will be like this? This must be explored.
What about non fund related votes? Maybe there is economics to all
decisions that can be voted by determining the price of choices and a
system of execution.

Makerspace tool breaks down. A goal could raise the funds to repair it
or replace it. But this might take a long time, in which many projects
would not get completed, it would be much better to be able to replace
or repair it quicker. To do that a fund would need to be established
ahead of time, as well as policy for repair/replacement would need to be
established ahead of time. Soon as a vote is passed that it is no longer
functioning, a policy can automatically allocate funds, and then users
and companies can bid to replace or repair it. Bidding duration is also
established in the policy. Now a tool is back in order after just a few
days, since it does not need to wait for funding. Service providers
(those who will repair and replace it) are still competing with each
other to drive the price down by being able to bid. Bidding is open
within the community and accepted by community vote

What about combing both. So all goals can have bids. Anyone can bid on a
goal with a set price. Policy is then used to establish how a fund might
pay for that.

Thought: Why would users use their own funds if the community fund will
cover it? What if their funds are repaid with tokens, or just repaid
from the fund once the goal is completed? The point is that members will
vote with value, it is a stake in a goal.

Example, tool breaks. Goal to repair/fix it is started by a member.
Users can begin pledge to the fund. Other users or companies can begin
to bid. The fund reached 1000 coins. A reasonable bid comes in, at 2500
coins . The community votes and it passes to accept the bid. The users
that pledged the coins have their coins returned to them. Once the job
and goal is completed the community pays for the service from its fund.

#### What if a tool that no one uses, but 1 person, breaks?

Should the community fund cover it, even though it does not benefit most
of the community? What if the community fund didn\'t cover it, then is
that person expected to cover the cost? Even though other members might
use it in the future? Neither of these options are ideal? But a blend of
both might be.

In a simplified in person situation, two people pitch in for a tool, it
breaks, but only one uses it. The other might not want to pitch in to
repair since they dont use it, even though later on they might need it.
The one person covers the cost, but makes a deal if the other uses it
then they have to cover half of the repair cost. This might be a good
example compromise that doesn\'t involve a fund.

Another argument is that by being in a group, the idea is that u commit
to supporting each other, whether you use the tools or not. And so
splitting the costs of repair is expected, or even a fund to do this.

In Fact the whole idea of a community fund is not ideal. Having excess
funds should be avoided. Funds should always be flowing, not sitting.
Resources should always be put to use, not accumulated. And on rainy
days people come together.

In this case then when an expensive tool breaks, that many members use,
they can all begin to pledge funds for a repair/replace fund, while
receiving bids.

There might be better policies suited to different types of communities.
But the idea could be to find something that works with most for now,
and introduce new features and policies later on. A fund can always be
added later.

Cases:

1.  Tool breaks, someone was not at fault

    a.  Goal started to remedy, funds get placed, bids get made.

2.  Tool breaks, someone was at fault. After an inspection of the break
    it is voted that the tool was broken at fault, no appeal is made.

    a.  Deposit from the individual is not enough to cover fixes.

    b.  Deposit from the individual is not enough to cover fixes. Goal
        must raise the rest

#### Differences between a reserve fund and fund raising

The both have the same purpose, to fund something. But a reserve fund
gives you access to funds faster.

If a reserve fund is opted for, then a limit policy is recommended, so
that it does not grow endlessly but has a definable end limit, possibly
based on the asset with the greatest value, something similar to an
insurance policy. Once this limit is reached, the fund-time accumulation
from members can be lowered to match that of expenses only. Maybe the
reserve fund limit can scale with group count, so that new members must
buy into the reserve fund to have its value balanced across all members.
When a member leaves, the value they contributed to the fund can either
be returned, or be distributed to existing members proportional to the
amount the contributed.

### Recurring Costs & Currency

An expense fund must be maintained in a currency which has equal value
to the currency the expenses are paid in. e.g. The landlord for rent
charges in \$, and so a bank account with \$ can be maintained for the
expense fund, or a coin account that matches \$ in Value, such as a
tether for example, or a coin that guarantees "only up" (through asset
backed community voted loans, which could eventually be an open project
itself)

Members buy tokens at \$1 per token

### OwnerShip & Asset Value

Who owns the tools? No one, the community as a whole does, but If the
makerspace shuts down, where do the tools go? Are they sold? Who gets
the value of the tools? Who gets the reserve fund? Who gets the value of
all the assets?

One option is that the members get the tools or value of the tools.
Another option is that whoever has tokens gets the value of the
community liquidation proportional to their token amount.

### What about multiple locations?

Can a member go to any location? Ideally yes, but what are the
implications? Liability would be the same, the deposit would be the
insurance policy. Maybe when multiple locations decide to join, a joint
reserve fund can be established, sitting on top of the local reserve
fund. Any issues caused by a non local member would be covered by this
fund.

There would be indoor member limits to the space, and local members
would get priority to access.

### Tiers

Tools can be in different tiers based on their training requirement,
danger and cost. Each tier has a higher deposit cost and training
requirement.

### Injury

What happens with liability in the case of injury. Who is liable in case
of injury from unsafe conditions. Since this is a community, everyone
has equal responsibility. Legally a waiver can be signed for common
dangers depending on the tier of tool being used and the general
workshop.

## Affordable Electric Car: A tangible product case study

This will follow the entire lifespan from conception, design, prototype,
manufacturing, service, logistics.

### Conception and Design

A discussion begins about an affordable electric car, the goal is to
come up with a design that will allow for the most affordable electric
car while meeting all legal standards of most provinces/states.

Various users start proposing ideas. Good ideas begin to get upvoted in
various categories. Some users begin to donate to the goal fund.

Users begin coming up with renders, schematics, mechanical designs,
aerodynamic designs, etc. Some are doing it because they are passionate
about the goal and subjects, some are doing it because the reward of the
goal fund is increasing and they want a cut for contributing voted value
once the goal is voted as completed.

After some time there is enough content to package various full designs.
Several users begin to create packages forking previous packages. Some
users just contribute suggestions for changes to existing packages.

In the end it came down to three different designs, one for urban
travel, another for urban and highway, and another sport version.

Since the goal is now close to completion, users begin to vote on what
subject tags the fund should be distributed by. Many tags are voted, the
top 3 are, #car design, #car mechanics, #electric car, but there many
more.

As soon as more than 50% of subscribers with experience in those tags
vote that the goal has passed, the funds will be distributed in
proportion to the amount of votes in each tag each contributor received.

At this point there are 3 electric vehicle design projects that emerge,
users begin to fund the projects monthly in order to support future
development. The same voting to fund distribution delivers the funds
monthly to the contributors. So developers are incentivized to compete
for best value of contribution the fastest, to get the most votes.

### Prototypes

Several groups in maker spaces begin to race to build prototypes.
Several crop up around the world, pictures, videos emerge with the
prototype functioning. Issues and proposed fixes are reported. All of
these contributions by the groups result in donations as well as a cut
from the design funds from the projects they are reporting to. A loop
between designers and prototypers emerges of requesting certain tests,
and reporting on feedback.

Sometimes a very costly goal is requested by the community, such as
crash testing several vehicles. No maker space group takes on the
challenge as a result. The goal can then get its own fund, and now maker
space groups can begin to outbid each other for a proposed fund to build
a crash test and perform it.

The goal fund is growing slowly, and so a user proposes that 30% of the
monthly fund be diverted into the crash test fund, the
members/subscribers vote to pass it.

After several iterations of design changes and prototype testing no
major problems are reported for many weeks. The prototypes are enjoying
a well functioning electric car and they are constantly posting about it
getting votes and donations for their contributions.

### Manufacturing

At this point users around the world begin placing deposits into
manufacturing funds for their local area. E.g. Toronto Canada has 10,000
users each placing \$1000 deposits in the local fund. Users from
anywhere in the world can contribute to the fund, as long as they are
willing to commute to pick up their vehicle when it\'s done. The fund at
10 million plus its growth rate of 2 million a week becomes very
attractive to groups and traditional manufacturers.

Each deposit buys a token. When a manufacturer is given tokens, they can
exchange them for their fund equivalent.

Manufacturers can bid against the fund, they can state their company
history, past examples, price per vehicle, cost per vehicle, delivery
time and required order amount before they can start production. Users
can accept a bid of their choice, and when the required order amount is
reached, the production can begin.

The deposits can be set as required upfront for production, or held
until delivery. The second option will provide more consumer trust, but
the first will help smaller production startups with costs.

If it is the first time a specific manufacturer has created a project,
then consumer confidence will also go up in the bid if they specify they
will offer a discount for the first x units to be reviewed. E.g. The
first 20 units will be 30% off to reviewers that can provide feedback
before the rest of the units are mass produced.

When the first batch of mass produced units are ready, the users will go
to pick up the electric car, and transfer the rest of the cost to the
manufacturer.

### Service

At a bare minimum if a user has a problem they can create a goal for
local help with the fix, periodically adding money into the fund to
raise interest, (anyone can donate to their goal also).

Users could also create training material to teach existing shops how to
service the vehicle, shops could list themselves on the project page
under local service centers in order to gain a new set of customers.

In order to inspire confidence in the project, a local group or company
can create a service to cover any non-fault issues to the car for up to
x years.

#### Resale

Some groups can choose to invest their own capital in order to build
several vehicles for resale in the traditional market, at say a car
dealership, there are no issues with that.

### Conclusion

In a way this is very much the way the world works today, using a
healthy free market to drive change. In other ways it\'s very different
than the world of today.

##### How it differs from today's world

1.  Users discuss and determine what they want vs Companies try to
    create a want through marketing

2.  Users can commit to a purchase before the item/service is completed
    vs needing a sales team to sell an item or service to a consumer.

3.  Users can take any role they are passionate and capable of at any
    point of an open project (an open/free market) vs they must rely on
    a company that handles every aspect of a product service inside a
    black box.

4.  Users no longer have to keep their ideas secret in fear of them
    being stolen by the bigger guy. Users are racing each other to share
    the best idea first so that they get the most votes and thus a
    bigger cut of the payout. In each purchase the user has the option
    of paying a % to the founders, this can also be voted as a mandatory
    cost. A user could gather enough shares through any contribution to
    make a comfortable living just off royalties.

## VegiBarter: A digital service case study

### Concept and Design

A user has an idea to create an app/service that allows neighbours to
exchange the vegetables they grow through a grassroots economy of scale
benefit. By having one neighbour specialize in a few vegetables, while
another specializes in a different set, they can become specialized in
their growth and reduce the total cost by having a higher volume. Any
abundance can be shared with a neighbour for a veggie token. A lettuce
can be worth 10 tokens, if its nice and green and a good size, or 5 if
it is smaller, while a tomato might be worth 20 tokens for a good
quality tomato, etc.

Other users upvote the idea, and many others begin to contribute more
ideas and suggestions. Some users begin to donate to the project, and
the contributors get the payout instantly for the value they provided
proportional to the votes received.

The main founder (user with the idea) creates a project out of the
original discussion thread/page, although anyone could have done this
also. The project has specifications as goals that are referenced in the
original discussion. Users begin to upvote the project and make
suggestions to what else to include from the original discussion.

### Design

The original user might have had a great idea, but they do not know how
to create it. The project begins to gain funding, and the funds trickle
down into each specification goal. As the funds reach limits, users can
begin to submit their submissions for approval voting. As the
submissions are accepted, and thus the goals completed, the funds are
delivered. It is a race to provide the best quality content and
submissions.

Some time later most of the goals are completed, some new one are added,
and the fund continues to grow from one time fund contributions or
periodic/monthly contributions.

Users vote to create a token with a defined value based on an assessment
of common vegetable costs at the time. The tokens are issued to all past
and future contributors. Any user that contributed content will receive
the typical funds for their content, and the equivalent token value, and
any user that contributed funds will get the equivalent token value.

### Prototype

A usable beta is finally achieved, and users begin to test it out in
their neighbourhoods, creating a feedback loop to the designers. With
each iteration a new version is released. Once bug reporting becomes
stagnant, the version is released out of beta. There is no launch party,
marketing, or sales needed. The app has already been out and has already
been growing.

### Service

When a user has a problem they just post a bug report, if it is a common
problem, the report might and solution might get many votes, giving a
greater payout cut to whoever solved it. If no one can solve it, a
funding goal might start as a bounty to the first person to start it.
