## Example: Maker Space

After some discussion and polls on the open forums, it is revealed that
a makerspace would be in demand in a city that does not have any, let\'s
call the city Oakville. An open company is formed. Discussions are made
about what it will initially offer (equipment, services, etc) and what
location within the city it will have, what square footage it will need.
Once all of those details are established through votes then an
evaluation is done by users submitting how much they would pay monthly
for such a service. Next a token is created and its value is voted in
based on the chosen evaluation. It is decided that 100 tokens a month
will pay for membership, therefore 100 tokens will have an estimated
value of \$50 based off the monthly membership cost estimate, and so the
token is worth 50 cents at the point of the initial token offering, but
it is discounted by 50% for the first month by vote to incentivize early
adopters. The token goes on sale and all the funds are raised in order
to cover rent for the first few months, and to get the equipment needed.
People all over the world can purchase the token, and they might because
they see the company evaluation and they know they can sell it back for
profit eventually. Doing this allows for quickly raising funds from
investors, who will only naturally act as silent investors, since there
is no ownership in an open system.

At this point the company makes a call for equipment to all cities
nearby through various marketplaces. The company votes that sellers can
take double the tokens in value. E.g. a seller can provide a jigsaw for
\$50 dollars cash or \$100 worth of tokens; 200 tokens. This
incentivizes members to act as procurers. Same goes for rent, a member
can rent their property for double the tokens of cash.

Once the maker space is operational, every member must pay monthly
membership fee in tokens. If they do not have tokens they can buy more.

The makerspace will have ongoing discussions about what tools, machines
or services they would like next. There will be many proposals made by
members and non members that just follow out of interest. E.g. a
discussion such as "let\'s get a metal 3d printer". There are several
ways the maker space can fund a new machine. Someone can create a bounty
for a metal printer. With a bounty anyone, member or non member can
donate. When the bounty grows to a certain amount, some creators might
propose building one as an open project, or someone can propose selling
a new/used printer to the company. These proposals would be voted by the
members. The other way would be to propose using the treasury reserve of
the company to make the purchase, or a combination of both, by using the
treasury to create a bounty.

The maker space will continue to operate through democracy and automated
by voted in smart contracts. Any change to policy or operations will be
executed by changing an existing smart contract or adding a new one.

Since everything is open, this makerspace can be used as a template for
other new emerging makerspaces, by just simply copying all the smart
contracts that operate the space and applying it to a new company.

## Applying Open Systems to Manufacturing

Completely open transactions and open systems interaction. Smart
contract for system and consumer policy interaction. Templates for
various manufacturing ventures with policy, protocol standards enforced
by smart contracts.

E.g. deposit first. Payments held in escrow. Claims handling and
processing through voting.

## Level of Open Systems Integration

A company can have a varying degree of integration and interaction with
the open systems standard and network.

A traditional company will have no integration with OS.

These are the various levels and key features:

1.  Company profile

    a.  users can comment, review

    b.  Users can make claims

    c.  Companies can create discussions, replies, gain merit and vote.

    d.  Donation features with open ledger

2.  Payment processing

    a.  Users can pay for or deposit towards goods and services

    b.  Escrow smart contract policies

    c.  Buyer and seller protection

3.  Funding

    a.  Companies can raise funds through token sales

    b.  Managing of token exchange

4.  Fully open

    a.  Company has no ownership

    b.  All actions are made through vote of members or public

    c.  Fully automated without leadership (can still have human
        resources)

The idea is for companies to transition into fully open as the
founder/owners are no longer able to act as founder/owners for various
reasons.

## Example: Manufacturing Venture

Due to a technology venture, a revolutionary electric car project was
successful and now many people are placing deposits into local
manufacturing bounties. Open Manufacturers have begun to set up to
produce the open source design in various parts of the world. In Toronto
there are already 2000 deposits, each being \$1000, totaling \$2
million, in a short time. Various manufacturing ventures begin
organizing and setting up around the Toronto area. At this point it's a
race to produce a prototype that gets reviews from test customers in the
area. Once confidence grows with a manufacturer, they might get most of
the deposits. Notice there is competition between manufacturing ventures
as to drive advancements in improving the process. Since everything is
open, the idea is to be first and to have the capability to sustain
momentum.

Once a vehicle is ready, the purchaser must escrow the full amount of
the car another \$14,000. When the vehicle is delivered, the full amount
is held in escrow for a month (predetermined period) in case there are
any claims made. After this period the company gets the full amount.

Demand continues to grow as consumer confidence grows in the
manufacturer. Now the company needs to look at expanding production
capabilities. They can consider more automation, human resources, new
locations, or even buying out competing operations.

#### How do buyouts happen with open companies?

Company A and company B are competing for customers.

Company A has more demand than supply, Company B has more demand than
supply. Then they are both interested in ramping up supply, but a buyout
is out of the question because they would also take on more supply which
does not solve the issue.

Company A has more supply than demand, Company B has more demand than
supply. Then B might want to buy out A since it will be able to produce
more while not increasing demand beyond supply. Company A might want to
sell since its not healthy.

Both A and B have more supply than demand. They are both unhealthy and
must ramp down as maybe the market is shrinking.

In these examples its really more about supply capability than supply,
since stock will be less of factor with open companies.
