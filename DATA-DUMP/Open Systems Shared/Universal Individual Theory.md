might be invested in. If it is investment, then entities may be non
physically invested in other things. E.g. an entity is invested in a
group, that has now changed its policy to one that is conflicting with
the entity.

It is then the investment, which is a plan over time for the growth of
value, based on an expected or speculative behavior. If that behavior
changes against policy then there might be damages.

Behavior that conflicts with policy can result in damages (a break in
social contract). Behavior that results in unconsented experience

## Certifications: The solution to international organization under no global authority

There have been many attempts at international organizations. Any time
two or more nations organize in some fashion, reach an agreement, or
sign a treaty, this is a form of international organization. When one of
these organization members backs out of a deal, there really is no
authority to force accountability. There have been attempts to govern,
direct, or influence the concept of a global state, but it has almost
always failed. By failed I mean it has resulted in a net loss to the
states including the people in the states, or a downright disaster as
with the UN attempting to govern conflicts.

Treaties might be the closest thing resembling certifications. Whereas
one nation expects that another nation will behave in a described way,
and thus modifies its behavior, and if either nation fails to behave as
expected, the other then backs out of its agreement and might also
change its behavior. A treaty in this context is then an agreement that
describes mutually expected behavior.

For example, a nation can promise not to be hostile if the other
promises not to be hostile. There is no authority enforcing that
promise, the next day they could go back to being hostile, and thus the
only value out of a treaty is the definition of the expected behavior;
the promise.

If there is a way to factor in trust, this further increases the value
of the promise, since it allows each party to get a qualitative
probability that the other party will uphold their promise.

If policy can be introduced that ensures interactions that could
demonstrate a failure of the promise are monitored, then a quantitative
probability of the above can be expressed.

Certifications then I propose are the formal definitions of a set of
expected behaviors defined. A certification can contain one or more
policies, which will outline the consequences for damages obtained from
breach of a promise. By having formal policies to deal with failure, it
increases the chances that human intervention will not escalate the
situation.

Behaviour and the environment changes over time, therefore the policies
of certifications might lose effectiveness over time. Therefore a formal
system for the ongoing improvement of certifications and the ongoing
assessment of the members and environment is required. To be able to
achieve this without a global authority will require a decentralized
social mechanic.
